  PCA's "Distractions" in the Thymus:
  The genes PCA picked (like Gm25801, Zfhx3, and Asxl3) are classic technical and structural artifacts:

   1. Nuclear/Splicing Noise: PCA is focusing on small nuclear RNAs (snRNAs). This suggests PCA's "Thymus signal" is
      actually picking up on differences in nuclear capture efficiency or mRNA splicing activity between batches. This
      is a common form of transcriptomic "noise."
   2. Gene Length Bias: Several of PCA's top genes are exceptionally large (>150kb). PCA is biased toward these genes
      because they naturally have more reads and higher variance. PCA is likely capturing transcriptional complexity or
      cell size rather than the biological effect of spaceflight.
   3. Local "Flukes": Because PCA only looks at the OSDR data, it has identified these high-variance genes as its
      "expert" markers. However, as we saw in the cross-validation, these genes don't generalize well because they are
      tied to the specific technical conditions of the experiments.

  Contrast with Binformer:
   * PCA is looking at "How much mRNA is being made?" (distracted by cell size and nuclear noise).
   * Binformer is looking at "What is the cell doing?" (focused on the core Mitochondrial and Ribosomal stress
     responses).

  Final Verdict:
  Binformer's pretraining on 20k mouse samples allowed it to "learn to ignore" these technical artifacts. It knows that
  snRNAs and large genes are often noise. Instead, it "searched" the Thymus data for the more subtle, but biologically
  meaningful, patterns it has seen before in other stressed tissues.

  This is the "Foundation Model Advantage": It uses its prior knowledge of biology to act as a noise-filter, allowing it
  to find universal signals that standard statistical methods like PCA completely miss.
